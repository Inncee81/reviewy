Great at first and looking forward to seeing how it lasts

Since this grow light is so new that it doesn’t have many reviews, let alone any long-term ones, I’m going to try very hard to update this review regularly. The public deserves to know!After getting a cheap LED grow light that fizzled out after only 4 months of use (light comes on but much weaker than when I first got it) I learned my lesson: there are only two kinds of LEDs, the good kind that lasts and the cheap kind.So I went looking for a really good LED grow light, between 150 and 300 watts, for about $100 or less. There where a few nice options in the $90 range, but I went with this one because my indoor setup is small (18” by 20”) and I wanted the dimmers since 300 watts might be a little too much and scorch the veggies, and I figured the independent veg/bloom dimmers where worth the little bit extra.The box was very well packed with dense foam and the hanging cables in a bag & the power cord packed in a separate compartment from the light. The foam even had a cutaway around the dimmer knobs so it wouldn’t press on them. Amazon, however, just used not-enough packing paper to fill the shipping box, letting the light shift around a few inches. Certainly not a deal breaker, just check your light ASAP to make sure it wasn’t damaged.Both dimmers turn smoothly and stay where you set them with the fans coming on at 30%, though note that there are two fans, one turns on for the veg dimmer, one for the bloom dimmer. Light is nice and bright. At the dimmest setting and using my green shades I gave the LEDs a quick glance to make sure all where running. They where. The manufacurer specifically warns against doing this but I needed to make sure it was working and I was using appropriate safty equipment.While the included hanging cables are very good, I prefer to use chains to hang lights as the links in the chain give you lots of options for exactly how high, where, and at what angle the light is. If you do this, make sure it’s good strong chain. Lamp chains work very well as long as you aren’t using very heavy lights and they’re available at most hardware stores. Plus, chains just plain don’t kink and fray like cables can.This round of growing has me starting 25 Komatsuna (aka Mustard Spinach) and 50 Magenta Spreen seeds. I spread them in moist good quality serilized growing soil in sterilized milk cartons with drainage holes (I’m thrifty, but careful) and set them in my seedling dome with it’s top vents closed and bottom vents open to keep in most of moisture but let a little fresh air in and the lights set about one inch from the top of the dome and closed up the grower, vents closed to keep the light and warmth in (the grower is separate from the seedling dome.) The manual for the light recommends 30% bloom & 80% veg at 14 hours per day for germinating and I see no reason to argue so that’s what the light and timer where set at. There are no numbers for the dimmers so you have to estimate what’s 80% but since the fan kicks on at 30% it’s pretty easy to do. Since my setup is enclosed I have a small fan to keep the air moving, and with no plants to turn it to I angled it to blow across the top of the light to help move the hot air. Any heat shortens the life span of LEDs and even a little bit more cooling can help. I didn’t have a lot of luck planting the spreen outdoors, most didn’t come up, so this is partly a test of those seeds too.They where put in yesterday late afternoon. This morning after the light had been on for several hours I checked on things. It was very warm inside the grower, which surprized me a little because I hadn’t been using the heating pad. It wasn’t too hot for the seeds, but it was near their uppper range for sprouting so I opened the vents on the grower and adjusted the fan to blow hot air out the upper vents. An hour or so later and the temp is just right for sprouting these seeds.Now, it’s wait and see. I’ll be updating.Right now, with all the vents on the grower open, it’s still warm inside, and this is fine for what I’m growing now but it’s going to be a challenge keeping the temp low enough for the lettuces and carrots I’ll be growing soon. I live in a chilly basement so don’t need an air conditioner but I’ll probably need to get another fan, which means more outside air coming in which means I’ll need two fly strips instead of one and more energy used. I really wish manufacturers would stop using infrared diodes in LED grow lamps. Really, I know radiant heat is good for most crops and it means it’s another band of light that makes a full spectum lamp sound more impressive, but lettuce and many other plants hate heat and they’re commonly grown indoors. There are heaters or lights that run hot, they are very easy to come by and we can add those to a setup if heat is needed. Just please, please, stop putting IR diodes in LED lamps. Many growers find them a waste of electricity and a problem to deal with.Another lesson I learned: If you’re starting out with small scale supplemental lighting/indoor growing and want to see if LED lights are for you, it’s probably cheaper and easier to use a few household bulbs with a good color balance than an LED specifically for growing because the bulbs work, are cheap, easy to find, last, and are still energy efficient. There are also E26 (standard socket) red/blue LED grow lights for if and when you want to upgrade, and if you find indoor growing isn’t for you you can just use the bulbs in your home.
