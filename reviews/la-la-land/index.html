Here’s To The Fools Who Dream

La La Land is one of the best films of 2016. Its seamless combination of music and storytelling is reminiscent of classic musicals from the 50s and 60s. The performances are top-notch and the two leads have an unmistakable chemistry together (helped by their previous on-screen romance in Crazy, Stupid, Love.) Their characters are flawed, but that’s what entices the viewer to join them on this emotional journey. The score and musical numbers are absolutely memorable, even after one viewing. City of Stars and Another Day of Sun are my personal favorites, but there are many others that are, not only fantastic songs in their own right, but combined with some potentially iconic scenes in the movie, paint a picture that’s difficult to forget.The cinematography is beautiful, the choreography is stunning, and the camerawork is breathtaking. And that’s what La La Land is; breathtaking. No, it’s not perfect, as the film does peak towards the middle and the second half re-uses music and ideas from the first half, and personally I found there to be a couple of questionable casting choices, but those are minor nitpicks in what is otherwise a miracle production. I am very thankful that, in a world where superhero films and action blockbusters rake in the most dough, there are still producers out there willing to fund risky projects that can either fall and be forgotten, or rise and become instant classics. La La Land is fire. See it.

“Here’s to the ones who dream”

“Here’s to the ones who dream/Foolish, as they may seem/Here’s to the hearts that ache/ Here’s to the mess we make.” This song - “Fools Who Dream” - sung beautifully by Emma Stone as Mia Dolan during her career defining audition, pretty much sums up the plot for the romantic drama at the heart of the film. It is a touching story that somehow you wish will end another way but you realize and accept that it won’t. But getting past the drama, this is one of those movie musicals - a throwback to the classics - that are produced so rarely in the modern era. And this is an outstanding musical that will compare favorably to such classics as “Singing in the Rain”, “South Pacific”, “Carousel”, “Oklahoma” and others. Both Emma Stone as Mia Dolan, the aspiring actress/singer/dancer; and Ryan Gosling as Sebastian Wilder, the aspiring Jazz musician she falls in love with, deliver outstanding, award-caliber performances. Gosling is no Fred Astaire or Gene Kelly on the dance floor and his singing voice is not the best; but not all male stars of the classic musicals could sing well either - Rex Harrison in “My Fair Lady” is a case in point. Stone, however, excels as both a singer and a dancer, who would have been a worthy partner for either Astaire or Kelly. I already knew she was a gifted actress, who I’ve loved since seeing her in “The Help”; now I know she’s also a gifted singer and dancer. If you’re like I am and you love well made movie musicals and the magic and nostalgic feeling they give you when you watch them, you will love this film. I’m hoping its success will encourage the production of more like it!
