Close–but no cigar

This came in a nice case, was relatively easy to use (once I figured out how to put in the battery),and was reasonably comfortable to wear. (I was worried at first, because some people complained it did not fit in their ear. I have trouble putting ear plugs in my left ear–the only one that works now–but the smallest earbud fir perfectly.) It also worked quite well, and battery life appears to be very good. It was a revelation to hear many things again. My hearing has been slip-slip-sliding away for years, and I didn’t notice the full extant of the loss.On the negative side:1) This is a simple amplifier of ALL sounds. Hence, a loud sound is much LOUDER.2) There is a lot of annoying high frequency sounds in a noisy place.3) I could not adjust the unit to compensate for these defects. The lowest volume setting was too loud for a noisy environment. There are four program settings, but they all sounded the same to me. None cut out the annoying high frequency noises.In a quiet area this hearing aid worked perfectly, and helped me to hear clearly, especially conversations. But as soon as I got into any kind of noise, I was bombarded with high intensity, unpleasant sounds. I either had to turn it off, or (more commonly) I pulled it out of my ear and stuffed it into my pocket. Or so I thought. Coming back from a trip I found my pockets empty–I had lost the hearing aid.I will not be buying another. After extensive research I have ordered a Siemens Lotus 23P. It costs nearly three times what the Britzgo did, but has features that will make it much more useful: digital signal processing, noise and feedback cancellation, and most importantly–automatic gain compensation/compression. (That is, soft sounds are amplified, but loud sounds are not, or less so. After all, I have no trouble hearing loud sounds. They do not need to painfully loud.)
