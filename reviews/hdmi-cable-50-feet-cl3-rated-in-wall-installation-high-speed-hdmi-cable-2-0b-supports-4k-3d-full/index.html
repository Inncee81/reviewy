In addition to audio, video, and general web browsing I also do a lot of high resolution gaming and needed a solution that would allow me to play at my desk or on my TV without losing any visual fidelity. I first tried Steam’s In-Home Streaming method, but there were noticeable moments of a/v latency. I then purchased a 50 foot HDMI cable from Amazon, which provided perfect sound and picture and ended up using that for over a year. But the cable was only just barely able to reach between the devices and had to be draped over the corner of the couch, so it wasn’t going to work long term. After doing more research online, I looked into a HDMI via Cat5/6 method and purchased a Sewell Extender set that advertised the ability to convert the signal from the source through HDMI to Ethernet and then back to HDMI at the receiver up to a length of 200 feet. However, this did not work at all for me and after a lot of troubleshooting and testing a number of cables/lengths, I decided to return it and continue looking for other options. That’s when I decided to take a gamble on this single 100 foot HDMI. I had stayed away from cables this long previously because it seemed that the general consensus was that the signal degrades after about 50 feet. But seeing as how this one had a booster built in and reading a lot of positive reviews, I decided to give it a shot. The package arrived very quickly and in good condition. Connected the cable, being sure to make note of the input and output ends, and the picture and sound came through perfectly without any signal degradation, artifacts, or latency. Works great! Works great if you know what you’re doing

Excellent cable, great competitive price, works excellent after proper setup.We had minor issues, but they were expected. 100ft HDMI cables are already stretching the limit for the spec and really require an active cable such as this. That said, we were not running the cable directly from the source to the display, but rather were doing an in-wall installation, where the source uses a cable to a wall jack, the 100ft cable was an internal wall run cable to another wall jack, and then another cable from the wall jack to the display. This means you are running MORE than the 100ft (2x wall plates that usually have a 6” female cable on the inside, and 2ft cables from source to wall and from wall to display). This means our total run was 105ft, plus 6 interchanges instead of 2. This can cause issues regardless of the cable you use, so I cannot knock the cable for it at all.Make sure you use the cable in the right direction (active cables regenerate a signal and “push” the data in a specific direction and are labelled “source” and “display/TV” on their respective ends), and double check that the source device you are using supports the resolution you are trying to output to the display (we were using a micro computer to power two displays in separate rooms and had to go into the BIOS and set which output was the main output to enable a larger resolution on one display than the other. Check with your manufacturer’s user manual for this info)
